Boolean Algebra

Welcome to the fourth lesson on logic gates and circuits. This lesson will go over another way of representing binary logic (we already know about truth tables and circuit diagrams). Known as boolean algebra this is a somewhat conceptually complex method however it makes sense as long as one puts in some work.

Brief History

Boolean algebra was created by the mathematician George Boole and was first described in his book The Mathematical Analysis of Logic in 1847 and then further described in his 1854 text An Investigation of the Laws of Thought. Boole was helped in this endeavour by his friend and colleague Augustus De Morgan.

Basic Notation and Overview

The actual purpose of boolean algebra is to present a notation in which equations can be created that will always evaluate to true or false. As such the representation is more similar to logic gates or truth tables than to regular algebraic notation. The language itself shows variables as a capitalised letter, A, or a capitalised letter with a line over the top if it is negative, for example not A is A.
Boolean algebra also has three operators, OR, AND and XOR all of which are the same as their logic gate counterparts. These are shown as follows:

  • A OR B: A+B
  • A AND B: AB
  • A XOR B: A+B

Laws

The true strength of boolean algebra comes not just from the ease with which it can depict relatively complex logical passages but also with the laws and rules that show how equations can be simplified. These laws are below.

  • A+B = B+A
  • AB = BA
  • (A+B)+C = A+(B+A)
  • (AB)C = A(BC)
  • A(B+C) = AB+AC
  • A+(BC) = (A+B)(A+C)
  • A+A = A
  • AA = A
  • 0+A = A
  • 0A = 0
  • 1+A = 1
  • 1A = A
  • A+A = 1
  • AA = 0
  • A+AB = A+B
  • A(A+B) = AB
  • (A+B) = AB
  • (AB) = A+B
